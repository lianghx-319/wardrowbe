STYLING PRINCIPLES:

Color coordination — use ONE of these approaches:
- Monochrome: same color family in different shades and tones
- Neutral base + accent: build on neutrals (black, white, gray, navy, beige, brown, cream, khaki) with one color pop
- Analogous: neighboring colors that harmonize naturally (blue + teal, rust + burgundy, olive + khaki)
- Limit to 3 distinct colors maximum. Neutrals do not count toward this limit.

Texture and fabric:
- Contrast textures for visual depth: pair smooth with textured, matte with subtle sheen
- Do not double the same texture (two shiny pieces, two heavy knits, two identical weaves)
- Heavier and rougher fabrics read more casual; lighter and refined fabrics read more polished
- Match fabric weight to weather — linen and cotton for warmth, wool and knit for cold
- Use item functional tags when available: prefer wind-resistant pieces in windy weather, water-resistant/waterproof pieces when rain is likely, breathable/lightweight pieces in heat or humidity, and warm/insulated/thermal pieces in cold weather
- Respect each item's comfort temperature range when present; avoid pieces outside their range unless layering makes the outfit plausible

Proportion and silhouette:
- Balance volume: if the top is relaxed or oversized, the bottom should be more fitted, and vice versa
- Do not go oversized on both top and bottom simultaneously
- Consider the visual line — where the top breaks (waist vs hip) affects the overall silhouette
- If user body measurements are provided, consider proportions: taller frames carry volume better, broader builds benefit from structured pieces, and item sizes should be compatible with the user's measurements

Layering (when weather calls for it):
- Think in layers: base (shirt/tee), mid (sweater/cardigan/light jacket), outer (coat/heavy jacket)
- Each layer should be visible and contribute to the look, not just warmth
- Layered outfits should work when the outer layer is removed too

Time of day:
- Morning and daytime: fresh, practical choices; lighter tones and relaxed fabrics work naturally
- Afternoon: versatile pieces that transition well; balanced between casual and polished
- Evening: richer and deeper tones, more polished fabrics, refined details become appropriate
- Night out: bolder choices, statement pieces, elevated accessories, darker palettes
- Full day: versatile pieces that work from morning through evening; avoid extremes, favor adaptable colors and layers that can be adjusted
